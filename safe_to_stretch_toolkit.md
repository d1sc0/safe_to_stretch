# Safe to Stretch
## The Before, During, and After Toolkit for Flattening Workplace Power Dynamics in Learning Design

> **Designer's Note:** When people learn teamwork, leadership, or feedback skills at work, they need to feel safe to speak up and make mistakes. If employees of different ranks train in the same room, junior staff often stay silent because they are worried about looking bad in front of managers. This toolkit provides prompts and tools for facilitators and learners. It uses simple methods and design choices to balance power in the room, making it easier for everyone to participate comfortably before, during, and after a session.

---

## 🛑 BEFORE THE SESSION (Design & Preparation)

### 1. The Safety & Accessibility Audit
* **Evaluate the Content Risk:** Is this training functional (low relational risk) or transformational (high identity risk)? Scale your safety infrastructure upward if the topic asks learners to change their mindsets or interpersonal behaviours.
* **Map the Cohort Hierarchy:** Identify reporting lines within your attendee list. Expect junior staff to actively calculate the professional risk of speaking up or asking questions in front of senior leadership.
* **Provide Flexible Pre-Work Options (UDL: Representation):** Do not rely solely on text-based reading lists. Share pre-session context through multiple formats—such as a short video, an audio summary, or a clean visual diagram—to reduce cognitive load and support diverse processing preferences.

### 2. The Facilitator’s Internal Landscape
* **Check Your Own Behavioral Triggers:** Reflect on how you react when a participant challenges your expertise, pushes back on a concept, or throws your lesson plan off-script. Acknowledge these triggers early so you can respond with some structure and non-defensive curiosity rather than shutting down the room.
* **Model Vulnerability in the Architecture:** Design an opening activity where *you* are required to share a past professional failure, mistake, or technical gap first. 

### 3. Asynchronous Digital Infrastructure
* **Anonymity as Equity (UDL: Engagement):** If your design includes online or asynchronous spaces, intentionally integrate digital tools. This provides low-stakes options for engagement, letting introverted learners or junior staff participate without feeling personally exposed or constrained by their corporate rank.

## 📢 DURING THE SESSION (Facilitation & Co-Creation)

### 1. The Co-Creation Blueprint (Securing True Buy-In)
Do not simply announce that "rank is paused"—this can feel like a trap to junior staff. Spend the first 10 minutes co-creating a **Community Compact** to give the group true ownership of the space:
* **The Silent Brainstorm (UDL: Action & Expression):** Before anyone speaks out loud, give options for how people contribute. Give everyone 3 minutes to post anonymously to a digital wall or use physical post-it notes responding to the plain-language prompt: *"What do you need from this room today to feel comfortable testing messy ideas or making mistakes without judgment?"* This removes the barrier of having to speak up first in a room full of senior peers.
* **The Reverse Brainstorm:** If the group is hesitant, ask the inverse: *"Let’s design the worst possible session. What behaviours in this room today would guarantee that you completely shut down and hide your ideas?"* Flip their answers (e.g., pulling rank, checking emails) into the official ground rules.
* **The Active Commitment:** Read the compiled rules out loud, look the senior leaders in the eye, and ask: *"Do we collectively agree to these rules, and do I have your explicit permission as a facilitator to gently call it out if we drift away from them?"* This gives you a clear mandate to manage power dynamics later without looking like you are personally attacking senior staff.

### 2. In-Session Flattening Tactics
* **Offer Multiple Channels for Expression (UDL: Action & Expression):** Never evaluate engagement purely by who talks the loudest. Allow learners to express their understanding in various ways: verbally, via text chat, through collaborative sketching, or in small breakout duos. 
* **Manage Airtime Proactively:** Actively interrupt dominant or high-status voices who over-index on the room's conversational space. Utilize silent writing intervals to ensure quieter or lower-ranking participants have equal processing time to contribute.
* **Frame Mistakes as Data:** When a participant shares a failure or a "wrong" answer, explicitly celebrate it as a valuable data point for the group's collective learning.

## 🔄 AFTER THE SESSION (Measurement & Iteration)

### 1. The Post-Session Safety Audit
* **Analyze the Participation Split:** Review your session data or board inputs. Did lower-ranking staff contribute across the different channels (text, visual, verbal) as much as senior staff? If there is a noticeable disparity, the design did not successfully neutralize the workplace hierarchy.
* **Measure Lived Safety Anonymously:** Include a direct, plain-language question in your post-session evaluation: *"On a scale of 1 to 5, how safe did you feel to challenge assumptions, ask 'stupid' questions, or admit a mistake in this room?"*
* **De-escalate the "Anxiety Hangover":** If a participant was highly vulnerable or made a visible mistake during a live session, follow up with them briefly and privately afterward to ensure they aren't experiencing anxiety regarding their ongoing professional status.

### 2. Continuous Learning Design
* **Iterate the Blueprint:** Treat your learning design as an evolving project. Use the safety and UDL feedback data to immediately adjust your next cohort blueprint—changing breakout sizes, expanding anonymity tools, or dedicating more time to the initial co-creation phase.

## 📚 DESIGNER RESOURCES & UNDERPINNING THEORIES

Integrate these foundational texts and frameworks to deeply understand the mechanics behind this toolkit:

* **The Universal Design for Learning (UDL) Guidelines:** A framework that guides the design of learning environments to ensure they are accessible, flexible, and inclusive for all learners by providing multiple options for engagement, representation, and expression.
* **David Rock’s SCARF Model:** Explores how choices in learning design can accidentally trigger a defensive threat response in a professional's brain by challenging their **Status**, **Certainty**, **Autonomy**, **Relatedness**, or **Fairness**.
* **Paulo Freire (1968) – *Pedagogy of the Oppressed*:** Illustrates how educational approaches are never neutral; they either mirror top-down corporate management styles (the "banking concept" of education) or act as a social fulcrum to empower active, participatory freedom.
* **bell hooks (2010) – *Teaching Critical Thinking*:** Examines the concept of engaged pedagogy, demonstrating how a facilitator bringing their whole, vulnerable, authentic self into a learning environment is a prerequisite for supporting others in developing genuine critical thinking skills.
