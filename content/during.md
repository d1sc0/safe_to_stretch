# 📢 DURING THE SESSION (Facilitation & Co-Creation)

### 1. The Co-Creation Blueprint (Securing True Buy-In)

Do not simply announce that "rank is paused" or "this is a safe space"—this can feel like a trap to junior staff. Spend time at the start of the session co-creating a **Session Charter** to give the group true ownership of the space. You can suggest some basic ground rules, but build agreement around these and actively ask for contributions from the group.

- **The Silent Brainstorm:** Before anyone speaks out loud, give options for how people contribute. Give everyone 3 minutes to post anonymously to a digital wall or use physical post-it notes responding to the plain-language prompt: _"What do you need from this room today to feel comfortable testing messy ideas or making mistakes without judgement?"_ This makes it easier for people to share without having to speak out loud first in front of senior managers.
- **The Reverse Brainstorm:** If the group is hesitant, ask the inverse: _"Let’s design the worst possible session. What behaviours in this room today would guarantee that you completely shut down and hide your ideas?"_ Flip their answers (such as pulling rank or checking emails) into the ground rules.
- **The Active Commitment:** Read the compiled rules out loud, look the senior leaders in the eye, and ask: _"Do we collectively agree to these rules, and do I have your explicit permission as a facilitator to gently call it out if we drift away from them?"_ This gives you a clear agreement to manage power dynamics later without appearing to target senior staff.

### 2. In-Session Flattening Tactics

- **Offer Multiple Channels for Expression ([(UDL: Action & Expression)](https://udlguidelines.cast.org/action-expression)):** Never judge engagement only by who talks the loudest. Allow learners to express their understanding in different ways: speaking out loud, typing in a text chat, sketching ideas, or talking in pairs.
- **Manage Airtime Proactively:** Gently interrupt dominant or senior voices who take up too much discussion time. Use quiet writing times to make sure quieter or lower-ranking participants have time to think and contribute.
- **Frame Mistakes as Data:** When a participant shares a failure or a "wrong" answer, celebrate it as a valuable point for the group's collective learning.
